How Much is Poo Much: Fecal Bacteria in NYC's Private Beaches

By Anny Oberlink

What better way to spend a hot, sweaty, July day than by cooling off in one of New York City’s many beaches? For some of the private beach clubs, however, you might want to second guess your dip after heavy rainfall.

The Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (DOHMH) routinely collects and tests NYC’s beaches for levels of Enterococci bacteria—bacteria that live in the guts of humans and mammals, and can cause vomiting, diarrhea, nausea and other infections if digested. The DOHMH guidelines state that from a mean of five samples, 35 or less Colony Forming Units (CFU) per 100ml of water is considered safe for swimming, 35 – 104 CFU is an advisory and the beach may close if conditions persist, and anything above 104 CFU is considered hazardous and swimming or wading is restricted.

During heavy rainfall, NYC’s combined sewer systems can back up and cause a mix of stormwater and untreated sewage to flow directly into the city’s waterways.
